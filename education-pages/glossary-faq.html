HUD Glossary

42 essential terms every HUD home buyer should know.

Addendum

Supplemental document required as part of the HUD contract package.

All Bidders Period

Listing phase when both owner-occupants and investors may submit bids. Follows the exclusive listing period. See Program Rules.

Appraisal

Professional valuation ordered by a lender during the financing process to determine the property's market value.

As-Is

Property sold in current condition with no repair negotiations. All HUD homes are sold as-is.

Backup Bid

Secondary offer held in case the primary contract fails or is not completed.

Bid Acceptance

Official notice from HUD that your offer has been accepted. Triggers the contract package deadline.

Bid Period

The designated window during which offers can be submitted on a HUD listing. Varies by listing type and phase.

Case Number

Unique identifier assigned to each HUD property. Used to track the listing throughout the sale process.

Certified Funds

Guaranteed payment form such as a cashier's check or money order. Required for earnest money deposits.

Closing

Final settlement where ownership officially transfers from HUD to the buyer.

Closing Costs

Fees and expenses beyond the purchase price paid at closing, including title insurance, recording fees, and lender charges.

Commission

Compensation paid to the selling broker, typically funded by HUD as a percentage of the sale price.

Contract Package

Full documentation required within approximately 48 hours of bid acceptance. Includes signed contracts, earnest money, and supporting documents.

Deed

Legal document transferring property ownership from HUD to the buyer at closing.

Earnest Money Deposit

Certified good-faith deposit required at the time of offer. Amount varies by purchase price. See Program Rules for typical amounts.

Exclusive Listing Period

Initial listing phase reserved for owner-occupant buyers only. Investors cannot bid during this window. See Program Rules.

Extended Listing

Properties that remain available for offers after the initial bid period closes without an accepted offer. Bids may be reviewed daily or on a first-come, first-served basis.

FHA Loan

Government-backed loan commonly used for HUD home purchases. Requires lower down payments than conventional loans. See Financing Guide.

FHA 203(k)

Rehabilitation loan allowing renovation costs to be financed into the mortgage. Available in Standard and Limited versions. See Financing Guide.

Good Faith Deposit

See Earnest Money Deposit. Alternate term commonly used by buyers searching for HUD bidding requirements.

HUD

U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development. The federal agency that manages the sale of FHA-foreclosed properties.

HUD Homestore

Official website (hudhomestore.gov) where all HUD properties are listed for sale. The only authorized source for HUD listings.

IE Status (Insured with Escrow)

Property eligible for FHA financing but requires a repair escrow to be established after closing. See Case Status Explained.

IN Status (Insured)

Property eligible for FHA financing without a repair escrow requirement. Generally in better condition than IE properties. See Case Status Explained.

Inspection Period

Window of time during which the buyer may conduct a property inspection. HUD does not make repairs based on inspection findings.

Investor

Buyer purchasing a HUD property for rental income, resale, or other non-owner-occupant purposes. See Program Rules.

Listing Agent

The real estate broker or agent representing HUD in the sale of the property.

Listing Price

The price set by HUD based on an independent appraisal. May be adjusted if the property does not sell.

NAID

Name and Address Identifier. Broker authorization number required to submit bids on HUD properties.

Net to HUD

The amount HUD receives after allowable closing costs, commissions, and other deductions. This is how HUD evaluates bids — the highest net wins.

Owner-Occupant

Buyer who intends to use the HUD property as their primary residence for a minimum of 12 months. See Program Rules.

Owner-Occupant Certification

Signed attestation that the buyer will occupy the property as a primary residence. False certification carries federal penalties.

Pre-Approval

Verified lender approval letter required to support a financed offer on a HUD property. Stronger than a pre-qualification. See Buyer's Guide.

Proof of Funds

Documentation verifying cash purchase capability. Required for all cash offers on HUD homes.

Property Condition Report

HUD's disclosure of known property conditions. Not a substitute for a professional inspection.

Repair Escrow

Funds set aside after closing to complete required repairs on IE-status properties. See Case Status Explained.

REO (Real Estate Owned)

Property owned by a bank or lender after a failed foreclosure auction. Distinct from HUD homes in process and rules. See HUD vs REO.

Title Insurance

Insurance policy protecting the buyer and lender against defects in the property's title history.

UI Status (Uninsured)

Property not eligible for standard FHA financing due to condition or other factors. May require cash, 203(k), or alternative financing. See Case Status Explained.

UK Status (Unknown)

Property with an unknown condition classification. Buyers should exercise extra due diligence. See Case Status Explained.

VA Loan

Department of Veterans Affairs mortgage available to eligible military service members. Can be used on qualifying HUD properties. See Financing Guide.

Winterized

Property that has had its plumbing drained to prevent freeze damage. Common for vacant HUD properties in colder climates.

Frequently Asked Questions

25 answers to the questions HUD home buyers ask most.

Do I need a real estate agent to buy a HUD home?

Yes. All HUD bids must be submitted through a HUD-registered broker with an active NAID. Buyers cannot submit offers directly to HUD.

Can I submit a bid directly to HUD?

No. All bids must go through a HUD-registered real estate broker with a valid NAID number. See Program Rules.

When is earnest money due?

Your broker must have certified funds ready at the time the offer is written. If accepted, the full contract package is typically due within 48 hours.

Are personal checks accepted for earnest money?

No. Only certified funds such as cashier's checks or money orders are accepted for HUD earnest money deposits.

Does HUD negotiate repairs?

No. All HUD homes are sold as-is. HUD will not make repairs or offer credits based on inspection findings.

What happens if my financing falls through?

Your earnest money may be forfeited depending on contract contingencies and timing. Review your contract terms carefully before bidding.

Can investors bid during the exclusive listing period?

No. The exclusive listing period is reserved for owner-occupant buyers. Investors may bid once the all-bidders period begins. See Program Rules.

How long must an owner-occupant live in the property?

A minimum of 12 months. Owner-occupant certification carries federal penalties for false claims.

Can HUD lower the price if a property doesn't sell?

Yes. HUD may adjust pricing on properties that remain unsold after the initial listing period.

Are HUD homes good investments?

They can be, but success requires proper budgeting, appropriate financing, realistic renovation estimates, and a clear exit strategy.

How much does a HUD home cost?

HUD home prices vary widely based on location, condition, and local market values. Listing prices are set by independent appraisals and can range from under $50,000 to several hundred thousand dollars.

Can I get an inspection before bidding on a HUD home?

You cannot inspect the interior before submitting a bid. However, you can view the property exterior and review HUD's property condition report. If your bid is accepted, you typically have a window to conduct a full inspection.

How long does it take to close on a HUD home?

Most HUD transactions close within 30 to 60 days of bid acceptance, depending on whether you are paying cash or financing. Cash closings can happen faster.

What credit score do I need to buy a HUD home?

There is no credit score requirement from HUD itself. However, if you are using an FHA loan, most lenders require a minimum score of 580 for the 3.5% down payment option, or 500 with 10% down. See Financing Guide.

Can I use a VA loan on a HUD home?

Yes, VA loans can be used on HUD homes that are in satisfactory condition. The property must meet VA minimum property requirements, and not all HUD listings will qualify.

What happens after my HUD bid is accepted?

You will receive a bid acceptance notice. Your broker then has approximately 48 hours to submit the complete contract package, including signed documents and certified earnest money. See Buyer's Guide.

How do I find HUD homes for sale?

All HUD properties are listed on HUD Homestore (hudhomestore.gov). HUDPRO also provides enhanced listing data, market insights, and search tools to help buyers find and evaluate HUD properties.

Can I bid on multiple HUD properties at the same time?

Yes. You may submit bids on multiple properties simultaneously. However, if multiple bids are accepted, you are obligated to complete each contract or risk losing your earnest money.

What is the difference between an exclusive listing and an extended listing?

An exclusive listing is in its initial bid period with a set deadline. An extended listing has passed its initial period without an accepted offer and is open for immediate bids on a first-come, first-served basis. See Program Rules.

Do I have to pay closing costs on a HUD home?

Yes. Buyers are responsible for standard closing costs. In some cases, HUD may contribute toward closing costs depending on the listing terms, but this is not guaranteed.

Can I buy a HUD home if I already own another property?

Yes, but you cannot certify as an owner-occupant unless you intend to make the HUD property your primary residence. If you own other properties and intend to use this as an investment, you must bid as an investor during the all-bidders period.

What does "as-is" mean on a HUD home?

It means HUD will not make any repairs, offer repair credits, or negotiate on property condition. You are buying the property in its current state, and your bid should account for any needed work.

Can I see the inside of a HUD home before I bid?

Interior access is not typically available before bidding. You can view the exterior, review photos on HUD Homestore, and read HUD's property condition report. A full inspection can be done after bid acceptance.

What is a net bid on a HUD home?

HUD evaluates bids based on the net amount it receives after deducting commissions and any closing cost contributions. The highest net-to-HUD offer wins, not necessarily the highest purchase price. See Program Rules.

How much earnest money do I need for a HUD home?

Earnest money requirements vary by purchase price. Typically, it ranges from $500 to $2,000 for lower-priced properties and increases with higher purchase prices. Your broker will confirm the exact amount. See Program Rules.
